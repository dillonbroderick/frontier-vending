The U.S. Dollar

Where It Started

Following the ratification of the Constitution in 1788, the Mint Act of 1792 was passed. This act solidified the monetary system of the United States and declared the dollar to be the primary unit for the system.

Notable Events

In 1873, the Coinage Act was passed. The objective of this act was to shift the United States from a coinage system utilizing both gold and silver to a system relying entirely on the gold standard.

The Federal Reserve Act (also known as "The Fed") was passed in 1913. This act created a central bank to oversee the monetary system in hopes of influencing economic stability.

In 1971, the United States under President Nixon ended its use of the gold standard. This terminated the convertibility of the US dollar to gold. Additionally, this effectively ended the Bretton Woods system.

Where it is Now

Today, the US dollar's value is volatile, but it remains a primary and dominant currency around the globe. A key factor in the US dollar we know today is its involvement in oil exchange. Oil-producing countries are paid in US dollars which are more commonly known as petrodollars in this context. All oil-purchasing countries must acquire the US dollar in order to pay for oil which reinforces US's dollar's global dominance.
